Lab Scenario

Sarah's auto policy renews in 30 days

Click Send Renewal Notice below to POST a renewal event to your Webex Connect flow's webhook. Your flow will text Sarah's mobile (the number you enter) and walk her through the renewal options you built.

What this page actually does (and why it exists)

In production, a policy administration system (or a scheduled job in a CRM) would automatically detect that Sarah's policy is 30 days from renewal and POST a JSON event to your Webex Connect webhook. That POST is what kicks off the SMS conversation.

This lab page simulates that backend system. You're playing the role of Farmers' policy admin platform — manually firing the same HTTP request a real system would send automatically. This lets you trigger your flow on demand without needing to wait 30 days for a real renewal, and without setting up a backend integration during a 1-hour lab.

In production: Replace this page with a nightly cron job, a Salesforce/Guidewire trigger, or any system that knows policy renewal dates. The webhook URL, payload structure, and authentication stay exactly the same.

Copy this from the Inbound Webhook node in your flow (after you save the flow).

Why this matters: Every Webex Connect flow with an Inbound Webhook trigger gets its own unique URL. That URL is the public entry point — anyone who knows it can start your flow. In this multi-tenant lab, the unique URL is what isolates your flow from the other 9 participants sharing the same Connect tenant. Treat it like a secret.

Include country code with a leading +. Example: +15551234567

Why this matters: The phone number is the destination of the SMS your flow will send. In production this would be Sarah's real number, pulled from the policy database and included in the renewal event. For the lab, use your own mobile so you can read the texts and reply to them — you're playing both Farmers' system and Sarah. The +countrycode format (E.164) is required by carrier networks worldwide.

Sent as the key header. Your instructor will share this if needed.

Why this matters: Webhooks are publicly reachable on the internet. Without authentication, anyone who guesses your URL could trigger your flow (and rack up SMS costs). The Service Key is a shared secret sent in an HTTP header — Connect rejects any POST that doesn't include the right key. In production you'd always enable this; in the lab it's optional so you can focus on the flow logic first.
Payload (preview) — this is the JSON your flow will receive 
{
  "phone_number": "+15551234567",
  "policy_id": "FRM-101",
  "policyholder_name": "Sarah Smith",
  "vehicle": "2022 Toyota RAV4",
  "renewal_date": "2026-06-04"
}
Each field becomes a variable inside your flow that downstream nodes (SMS, Branch, AI Agent) can reference:
  • phone_number — the destination address for the SMS Node. Without this, Connect doesn't know who to text.
  • policy_id — included in the SMS body so Sarah can identify which policy is renewing (a real customer may have several).
  • policyholder_name — used to personalize the greeting ("Hi Sarah," instead of "Hi customer,").
  • vehicle — gives Sarah specific context so she trusts the message is real and not phishing.
  • renewal_date — could be referenced in branch logic (e.g. urgency tier) or surfaced to the AI Agent as policy context.
Hard-coding these in the trigger keeps the lab simple. In production, the policy admin system pulls them from the customer record at the moment the event fires.
Tip: If you don't see an SMS within ~10 seconds, check the response panel below for HTTP status. 401 = wrong Service Key. 404 = wrong webhook URL or flow not published. 200 = the request reached Connect; check the flow execution log next.
